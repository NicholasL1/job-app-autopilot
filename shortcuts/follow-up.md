# Follow Up

## Purpose
Draft a professional follow-up email for a job you applied to, striking the right tone: polite persistence, not desperation.

## How to Install
1. Open the Claude in Chrome extension
2. Go to Settings → Shortcuts (or "Saved Prompts")
3. Click "New Shortcut"
4. Set the slash command to `/follow-up`
5. Paste the prompt below into the body
6. Save

## Prompt

```
I need to write a follow-up email for a job application. Please ask me a few quick questions first, then draft the email.

**My communication preferences:**
- Tone: [paste your communication.tone from profile.yaml]
- Name: [paste your personal.name from profile.yaml]
- Email: [paste your personal.email from profile.yaml]

Ask me:
1. What company and role did you apply to?
2. When did you apply? (approximate date is fine)
3. Did you have any prior contact at this company — recruiter name, referral, informational chat? If so, who?
4. Have you followed up before, and if so, when?
5. Is there any new information you want to mention (a relevant project you shipped, a piece of news about the company, etc.)?

Wait for my answers before drafting.

---

Once I've answered, draft a follow-up email that:

**Timing guidance (use my answer to determine which version to write):**
- 5–7 days after applying: short check-in, restate interest
- 2 weeks after applying: slightly more direct, restate key qualification
- 3+ weeks: either one final note or recommend moving on

**The email should:**
- Be under 150 words — hiring managers don't read long follow-ups
- Open by referencing the specific role and when I applied
- State ONE reason I'm still genuinely excited about this opportunity (not generic flattery)
- Include one sentence adding value — something new, a relevant observation, or a specific question
- End with a clear but low-pressure ask (e.g., "Happy to answer any questions or provide more context")
- Match my tone preference
- NOT use: "I just wanted to check in", "I know you're busy", "circling back", or "touching base"

**After the draft, provide:**
- A suggested subject line
- The best time to send (Tuesday–Thursday, mid-morning, tends to get better response)
- Whether you'd recommend sending at all based on the timing (if it's been 6+ weeks, it may be time to move on)

Output the draft and guidance. Do not send anything.
```

## Tips
- One follow-up is usually enough. Two at most. After that, redirect your energy to other applications.
- If you have a contact name (recruiter, hiring manager), address them directly — it makes a big difference.
- "Adding value" in the email doesn't have to be big: mentioning a recent company blog post, a product launch, or a shared connection keeps it from feeling like a form letter.
- If the listing is taken down or the company has announced layoffs since you applied, that context changes the email. Mention it if you have it.
- Keep a record of follow-ups in your applications.csv (add a note in the notes column).
